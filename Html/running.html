  1. 5k

    • ¿What is a 5K run?

      • For new runners who want to train to complete a race, 5K competitions are an ideal starting point.
      They are races with a length of 5 kilometers where each runner can give their best, in a relatively
      short time and distance.
      There are many 5k events, ranging from popular races to highly competitive races for the most experienced.
      These events are very popular with both new and experienced runners, and are a great way to raise money
      for a good cause.

    • ¿Is it difficult to run 5 km?

      • If you're just starting out running, 5km might seem like a long distance, but there are plenty of
      tried and tested training plans, like the ones we show you HERE, that will get you from your couch to
      the finish line in a matter of weeks. Once you're comfortable with the distance, you can start working
      on improving your speed and the time it takes to cover those 5Ks.

    • Ultimate Guide to 5K Races

      • Walking is a great way to ease into 5K training. Smartwatches and fitness tracking tools are becoming an
      increasingly popular way to monitor fitness progress, with step counting being a key feature. Most fitness
      trackers recommend walking 10,000 steps a day, but even starting with around 5,000 steps a day can be a lot of
      progress toward your goal, whether it's losing weight or running that 5K.


  2. 10k

    • ¿What is a 10K race?

      • A 10k race has a distance of 10 kilometers. It is ideal for experienced runners looking for more of a challenge.
      It's the second most popular race after the half marathon and requires a level of fitness that balances strength,
      energy, and stamina.
      If you've already run a 5K and like to run longer distances, the 10K may be the ideal next step.
      Completing a 10km race is an achievement in itself, being able to be happy with your time no matter what. However,
      it is normal that you want to share with other runners, as well as with your previous best times.
      Your age, cardiovascular condition, and musculoskeletal health can influence your individual performance,
      but the average 10K time is 50 to 70 minutes.

    • Average time of a 10K race

      • Most runners who are reasonably fit and run an average of 25 to 50 km per week should finish this type of race in an
      average time of 50 to 70 minutes.
      More advanced runners usually finish in about 43 to 50 minutes. While exceptionally fit runners can average under 40
      minutes, while more casual runners can exceed 60 minutes to cover those 10 kilometers.

    • Other factors that affect the average time 10 K

      • Additionally, the average 10K time can depend on factors such as age, gender, and fitness level.
      Your musculoskeletal health also comes into play, so you need to take steps to reduce pain, avoid injury, and run with
      proper form. You must properly treat factors such as shin splints, plantar fasciitis, and runner's knee.


  3. 21k

    • ¿Do you know what a half marathon or 21K?

      • A half marathon, or 21K race, is a race that is exactly 21.0975 km long, half a marathon or 42K, which is 42.195 km.
      In the grandest scheme of all races, the half marathon is in the middle of the road for racing.

    • ¿Why run a half marathon?

      • A half marathon is a good distance to try new challenges after running other distances like 5K or 10K. There are few races
      between a 10K race and a half marathon. 15K races do exist, but they are extremely rare. A 21K gives you the chance to run
      half the distance of a marathon and experience double the fun.
      When you cross the finish line of the half marathon, you will have accomplished something that less than 90% of the world will
      ever do. This is a great achievement and the memory will stay with you for the rest of your life. After your first half marathon
      race, you'll probably be hooked and want to run your next race as soon as possible.

    • ¿How do I train for a half marathon?

      • Most new runners will need to spend 12-16 weeks training for a half marathon. If you plan on running the entire half marathon,
      you'll probably need to spend about 12 weeks training for race day. This period of time can be shortened, especially if you are already
      running distances of approximately 10 km or more.
      The weekly progression to train for a half marathon starts with running about 4 times per week. One of your race days will be called a
      long distance run or long run for short. The other three run days will be base mileage build days. It is unnecessary at this stage, especially
      for beginners, to include fast work days. You will have at least one rest day and two strength days combined each week.


  4. 42k

    • ¿Do you know what a 42K marathon?

      • A marathon is a race with a distance of 42.195 kilometers. Although marathons differ in their terrain and degree of difficulty, the distance is always 42.195 km.
      Legend has it that Pheidippides, a Greek messenger, ran 40 kilometers from Marathon to Athens to deliver the news of the Athenians' victory over
      the Persians in 490 BC. C. He arrived to declare "Nikomen", which is "We won" in Greek. The word is derived from the name of the goddess Nike (Victory).
      Today, you can run a marathon wearing Nike on your feet.
      The distance of the modern marathon became 42.195 km at the 1908 Olympics, where the marathon course was designed so that it could start at Windsor Castle
      and end at the Olympic stadium.

    • Preparing to run a marathon

      • Before trying to run a marathon, it's a good idea to run for at least 6 months and run at least 3 times a week. If you've never run a race before, you should
      probably start with a shorter race, like the 5K, 10K, or 21K half marathon.
      Once you've completed a shorter running distance, you'll be in a better position to decide to take on the challenge of running 26 miles.
      If you want to train for a marathon, it's important to follow a training schedule so you're properly prepared for the race, avoid injury, and feel confident
      when you reach the starting line.
      Having a training schedule to follow will also keep you motivated for the 4-5 months it will take you to prepare for the race. With a well-designed plan, you'll
      steadily increase the distance of your long run for the week, and then reduce your mileage in the later weeks.
      This training period also allows you to practice good hydration and nutrition to maintain your energy during the race. You'll learn how to avoid blisters, chafing,
      and other common marathon injuries during long training days.

    • Discipline

      • Training to run a marathon takes a lot of time, commitment, and determination. The most typical marathon training program usually involves at least six days per week of
      training. This type of program takes up a lot of time and makes it difficult to balance with work, family, social life, etc. However, training many days a week is not
      necessary and often ends up causing exhaustion and injuries.
